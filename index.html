about

Brian Kelly, the songwriter behind Oceanography, is living the modern American dream: he moved in with his mom in order to make his art. Kelly’s path — and much of what inspires his brooding American rock sound — mirrors that of every lost suburban twentysomething who moved to Brooklyn in the Aughts to play in bands and work artsy jobs till the money ran out.

When Kelly returned to his native Northern California in 2009, he began Oceanography as a recording project in his childhood bedroom, releasing a self-titled EP in 2011. From there, Kelly put together a live band to play several Bay Area festivals, including Mission Creek Oakland and the City of Oakland’s Art and Soul Festival, and began writing what would become his latest EP, Parachutes of Plenty.

Whereas Kelly may have been driven by nostalgia for Brooklyn indie rock on his debut EP, Parachutes of Plenty shows a newfound confidence in his songwriting. The songs are still catchy, but there’s more complexity: guitars are heavier and more distorted; drums play an integral role, adding drama when paired alone with Kelly’s voice, like on “Reggie Jackson”; and Kelly’s vocal intensity is gripping and world-weary, in the tradition of legendary rock vocalists like Ian McCulloch of Echo & the Bunnymen and David Bowie. The press has taken notice, too. The Bay Bridged named “Reggie Jackson” number two on its Best of 2012 list, and MXDWN praised Oceanography’s “epic live performance.” Kelly is playing limited West Coast engagements with his Oakland-based band and plans to release his first full-length album soon.
